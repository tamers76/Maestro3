Part 02

Why we ingest references — and what a "vector database" means

The two ideas that make Maestro trustworthy, explained without the jargon.

02
Grounding

Why do we "ingest" your reference materials?

Because we want every piece of generated content to come from sources you approve — your textbooks, papers and notes — not from whatever an AI happened to memorise.

Accuracy

Answers are drawn from real passages in your materials, dramatically reducing AI "hallucinations".

Traceability

Generated content can cite exactly which source and section it came from — defensible and auditable.

Academic ownership

The curriculum reflects your chosen body of knowledge, keeping the institution's standards intact.

Think of it like

Briefing a brilliant teaching assistant. Before they help, you hand them your reading list and say "only use these." Ingestion is how Maestro reads and remembers that reading list.

MaestroWhy ingestion
Grounding

What "ingestion" actually means

Ingestion is simply the process of reading your documents and organising them so Maestro can find the right passage instantly whenever it's needed.

1It reads
Maestro opens your PDF or Word file and pulls out the text.
2It tidies
It removes noise like page numbers and table-of-contents clutter.
3It splits
It breaks the text into small, meaningful passages.
4It remembers
It stores each passage so it can be searched by meaning, not just keywords.
The payoff

After ingestion, Maestro can answer "what does my course material say about X?" in a fraction of a second — and point to the exact paragraph.

Think of it like

A librarian who reads every book you donate, then files every paragraph by topic — so any idea can be retrieved on demand.

MaestroWhat ingestion is
Grounding

What is a "vector database"?

It's a special kind of memory that stores text by meaning, so Maestro can find related ideas even when the exact words don't match.

Ordinary search

Matches exact words. Search "car" and it misses a page that only says "automobile" or "vehicle".

Vector (meaning-based) search

Understands concepts. Search "car" and it also finds "automobile", "vehicle", and "motoring" — because they mean the same thing.

Think of it like

A map of ideas. Every passage gets a "location" based on what it means. Passages about similar topics sit close together — so Maestro can walk to the right neighbourhood and grab the most relevant ones.

MaestroVector database, explained
Grounding

Why meaning-based memory matters for your course

FOR YOU

Better matches

When Maestro drafts content for an outcome, it pulls the passages that are genuinely about that outcome — even if phrased differently.

FOR STUDENTS

Coherent content

Material reads as one connected body of knowledge, drawn from across all your sources, not a keyword jumble.

FOR THE INSTITUTION

Confident grounding

Combined with normal keyword search, you get the best of both: precise terms and conceptual coverage.

Under the hood (optional detail)

Maestro stores these "meaning maps" in PostgreSQL using an extension called pgvector, and blends meaning-based search with classic keyword search for the most reliable results. You never have to touch any of this — it just works.

MaestroIngestion overview
Ingestion · Step 1 of 7

Step 1 — Upload & link your sources

What happens

  • You add reference materials to a course — PDF or Word files, or a direct link to a PDF.
  • Maestro records each document's details and keeps the original safe.
  • You can add textbooks, lecture notes, papers — as many as the course needs.
Why it matters

This is where you define the boundaries of trust. Only what you upload becomes part of the course's knowledge base.

In plain terms

You're handing Maestro the official reading list for the course.

!Gate
At least one reference must be uploaded before Phase 1 can proceed — grounding is mandatory.
MaestroStep 1 · Upload
Ingestion · Step 2 of 7

Step 2 — Extract the text

What happens

  • Maestro opens each document and reads out the words — turning a PDF or Word file into plain, workable text.
  • The full text is stored so later steps can re-process it without re-uploading.

The value

A PDF is just a picture of words to a computer until it's "read". This step makes your content understandable and searchable.

In plain terms

Like transcribing a printed book into a document you can search, highlight, and copy from.

Behind the scenes

Specialised readers handle PDF and Word formats so layout quirks don't corrupt the text.

MaestroStep 2 · Extract
Ingestion · Step 3 of 7

Step 3 — Clean & split into passages

What happens

  • The text is split into small, self-contained passages of roughly a few paragraphs each.
  • Passages slightly overlap, so an idea spanning a page break isn't cut in half.
  • A clean-up filter discards junk — page numbers, headers, table-of-contents noise.
Why split at all?

Searching a whole 400-page book for one idea is wasteful. Small passages let Maestro return precisely the relevant paragraph.

In plain terms

Like cutting a long book into well-labelled index cards — each card holds one coherent idea.

MaestroStep 3 · Chunk
Ingestion · Step 4 of 7

Step 4 — Add context to each passage

What happens

  • Maestro writes a short "context header" for each passage — which source it's from, the section, and what role it plays.
  • This header travels with the passage so its meaning is never ambiguous.

The value

A lone paragraph saying "this increases by 8%" is useless without context. The header restores the "increases in what, from which chapter".

In plain terms

Adding a sticky note to each index card: "From Intro to Economics, Ch. 4, on inflation."

Smart & efficient

These context labels are reused (cached), so re-processing is fast and inexpensive.

MaestroStep 4 · Context
Ingestion · Step 5 of 7

Step 5 — Capture the meaning (embedding)

What happens

  • Each passage (with its context header) is converted into a "meaning fingerprint" — a numerical signature of what it's about.
  • Passages about similar topics get similar fingerprints.

The value

This is what enables meaning-based search — the magic from the vector-database section, now applied to your sources.

In plain terms

Giving every index card a precise "coordinate" on the map of ideas, so similar cards naturally cluster together.

iYou stay in control
Administrators choose the embedding engine (e.g. OpenAI or a local model) in the Admin Center.
MaestroStep 5 · Embed
Ingestion · Step 6 of 7

Step 6 — Store & index for instant search

What happens

  • Every passage, its context, its original text, and its meaning fingerprint are stored together in the database.
  • An "index" is built — like the index at the back of a book — so the right passages can be found in milliseconds.

The value

No matter how many documents you add, retrieval stays fast and the original wording is preserved for accurate citation.

In plain terms

Filing every labelled, coordinated index card into a smart cabinet that can find any card the instant you describe what you need.

✓Done once
After this, the document is fully "ingested" and ready to power your whole course.
MaestroStep 6 · Index
Ingestion · Step 7 of 7

Step 7 — Retrieve & ground (used every time content is made)

Whenever Maestro drafts something, it first fetches the most relevant passages and writes from those.

How it finds the best passages

It blends two searches — meaning-based (concepts) and keyword (exact terms) — then keeps only the strongest, most relevant results.

A safety ladder

Best Passages tied to this exact outcome

Next Any relevant passage from the course

Last resort AI's own knowledge — clearly flagged as not yet grounded

Why this matters to you

You always know where content came from. If a section relied on ungrounded AI knowledge, Maestro flags it so you can add a source or review it carefully.

MaestroNode Engine · Overview
Node Engine

What is a "mastery node"?

A mastery node is a single, learnable idea — the smallest meaningful unit a student should master before moving on.

Connected

Nodes link by prerequisite — "learn this before that" — forming a map of the whole course.

Adaptive

Some nodes are essential; others can be skipped if a student already shows mastery, enabling personalised paths.

Grounded

Each node's content is generated from the references you aligned to its topic — with citations.

Think of it like

A subway map of your course. Each station is one idea; the lines show the order. Students travel the route that fits where they're starting from.
